Knowledge GAP Dashboard


DATE:     June 2014 - August 2014

TYPE:     Work Project, EMC Corporation, Field Support Specialist team

SKILLS:     Contextual Inquiry, Text Clustering, Data Manipulation, Python Programming

OVERVIEW

Knowledge GAP (Gap Analysis Program) Dashboard is designed to improve the training / re-training to the customer engineers by applying text clustering technique to the data from portal system. The dashboard tends to help users find out what are the most frequent questions that customer engineers have. Therefore, the users could make training plan focus on these questions.

BACKGROUND

Field Support Specialist (FSS) team I worked for is the customer service team in the EMC Corporation. It is responsible for providing technical support for customer engineers who diagnose, implement, and install EMC software and hardware products on the customer sites. The team also provides training for customer engineers. I came into the team as a data analyst with a goal to help improve the training efficiency for the customer engineers.

Methodology

I conducted a contextual interview with my manager to articulate the project goal. During the interview I asked his expectation for the project deliverables, the target audiences for the project and the resources we current have including data source and the platform. After this interview, I defined that the final deliverable is a dashboard with a completely documentation, the audiences are the managers in FSS team, the data comes from FSS portal system and there is no existing platform on the team.

Next step for me is to explore the data dumps file from FSS portal system. I used Tableau and Python to explore millions of service requests that customer engineers submit in portal system. Finally I decided to apply text-clustering technique to the requests. By doing so, the requests could be divided into several groups that are defined as knowledge gaps that customer engineers have. During this process, I also found out ten valuable fields from one hundred fields for text-clustering and dashboard development.

I used Library NLTK, Scikit-Learn to complete data manipulation and text clustering, Matplotlib to draw histogram in the dashboard, wxPython to develop the dashboard interface and use py2exe to convert python script into the executable Windows programs.

Results

Dashboard Welcome Page

Histogram

Products histogram will help users generate general ideas about requests. For example, what are the top 10 products in the portal system and which activity the top 10 products receive most requests.

Knowledge Gaps Display Page

After users choose filters from left side and click find button, the knowledge gaps will show on the right side.

Knowledge Gap Detail

When click detail button, the dashboard will show which requests are contained in this knowledge gap.

Documentation

I wrote a complete document about dashboard architecture, design and usage. This document could be used for future maintenance and update.

Accenture Consulting


DATE:    August 2011 – April 2012

TYPE:     Intern Projects, Accenture Consulting

SKILLS:     Metrics design, Data Model, Communication

Shared Service Center

During the last year of my undergraduate studies, I landed a consulting internship with Accenture. As my first project, I was responsible for planning a Shared Service Center (SSC). We conducted a survey to get ideas about the comprehensive service information from customers. In the survey, our client provided score for each service from several aspects, for example, the volume of daily, the quality of the work, the management type of the work, and the functions of the job. Then I grouped them into value, potential and difficulty. Value means how much value this business will bring if it put into SSC. Potential presents the likelihood to bring the services to SSC. The difficulty means the difficulty of the works. We would calculate the average scores for these three sides. If all the average score is smaller than 2 we think this service should put into the shared service center. However, when we use average score, it could not well differentiate the services that should put into shared service center from which should not. Thus, I suggested assign each aspect of service with different weight, and then to calculate the weighted average for the value, potential and difficulty. The final results show this method works better.

Library Search Engine


DATE:    January 2013 – April 2013

TYPE:     Course Project, University of Michigan – School of Information

SKILLS:    Database Design, PHP, MySQL, HTML, CSS, JavaScript, Information Visualization

The project goal is to build a search engine for library, and find out which algorithm is better for library search system. In the project, we compared the BM25 and Language Model (Bayesian smoothing using Dirichlet priors) and see which one is better.

We use “Harvard Library Bibliographic Dataset” (http://openmetadata.lib.harvard.edu/bibdata), which contains over 12 million bibliographic records for materials held by the Harvard Library. Since we have limited compute resource, we only used 0.1 million of them.

We decided to use Lucene 4.2 (which was released on Marth 11th, 2013) to build our search engine. The entire search engine used two search algorithms: BM25 Similarities and Bayesian smoothing using Dirichlet priors Language Model Similarities. We chose ten topics for search engine evaluation. Among the algorithms we tested, We found that the system with BM25 without adjusted weight has the best performance.

Michigan Club Council


DATE:    September 2013 – December 2013

TYPE:     Course Project, University of Michigan – School of Information

SKILLS:    Project Management, Contexture Inquiry

The project is focus on improving communications involving the University of Michigan Alumni Association Clubs Council. The Clubs Council is a 13-14 person volunteer group that was set up to aid the Alumni Association in its interactions with the roughly 70 University of Michigan Alumni Clubs that exist throughout the United States. The Alumni Association supports and funds the alumni clubs as they hold activities for their alumni throughout the year.

To gather information we interviewed eight alumni network members, including AA employees, CC members from both Virtual and Metro Clubs, and Alumni Club presidents. We also observed a semiannual meeting of the CC. After each interview we collectively analyzed our data, evaluating the organizational structure, culture, and communication practices.

Methodology

Finally we provided three main recommendations based on the findings. The first is to clarify the roles of the CC (Clubs Council), the second is that the individual clubs should schedule a designated amount of time just for CC business and information into their meetings, and the third is to reorganize Virtual Regions by proximity instead of by club size.
